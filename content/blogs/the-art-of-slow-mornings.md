title: "The Art of Slow Mornings"

There is something quietly radical about refusing to rush.

Most mornings, the world asks us to wake up already behind — notifications stacked like unpaid bills, calendars dictating the pace before our feet even touch the floor. I used to treat mornings like a sprint: alarm, shower, coffee, laptop, done. Efficient, maybe. But nourishing? Not even close.

## A different ritual

Last month I tried an experiment. No phone for the first forty-five minutes. Just light through the window, the smell of coffee blooming slow in the kitchen, and a notebook that didn't care about deadlines.

The first week was uncomfortable. My thumb kept reaching for a screen that wasn't there. By the second week, something shifted. Ideas arrived softer. Problems that felt urgent at midnight looked almost funny in the amber hour.

### What I actually do now

1. Wake without checking messages
2. Drink water before coffee (cliché, but my body stopped arguing)
3. Write three lines — not goals, just observations
4. Walk around the block, even when it's raining

> The point isn't productivity. It's presence. A morning that belongs to you, even briefly, changes the texture of everything that follows.

## On building things slowly

I've been building this blog the same way — not as a launch, but as a place to leave small truths. Notes, really. Fragments of what I'm learning about code, design, and the strange joy of making things on the internet.

If you're reading this, I hope you find a quiet minute today. Make tea. Open a window. Let the day arrive instead of chasing it.
